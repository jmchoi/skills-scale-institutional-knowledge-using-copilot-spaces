# OctoAcme Roles and Personas Onboarding Guide

This guide helps new team members quickly understand the eight OctoAcme roles, when to involve each one, and how to collaborate effectively across functions.

For full role definitions see [octoacme-roles-and-personas.md](octoacme-roles-and-personas.md).  
For interaction mappings and RACI details see [octoacme-roles-interaction-matrix.md](octoacme-roles-interaction-matrix.md).

---

## When to Involve Each Role

| Role | Involve When… |
|---|---|
| **Sponsor** | Major scope, budget, or priority decisions need executive approval; a blocker cannot be resolved at the team level |
| **Product Manager** | Defining feature requirements, adjusting priorities, or measuring outcomes |
| **Project Manager** | Scheduling, risk tracking, cross-team coordination, or status reporting |
| **Developer** | Estimating effort, designing solutions, or reviewing technical feasibility |
| **UX Designer** | Designing user interactions, validating usability, or evaluating design fidelity |
| **QA Lead** | Planning tests, assessing release readiness, or investigating escaped defects |
| **Scrum Master** | Removing team blockers, improving processes, or facilitating sprint ceremonies |
| **Technical Writer** | Creating or updating user guides, API docs, onboarding content, or release notes |

## How to Collaborate with Each Role

### Sponsor
- Prepare concise status summaries; focus on business impact and decisions needed.
- Bring escalations with a recommended path forward, not just a problem statement.
- Respect their time—limit touch-points to milestones, critical risks, or scope changes.

### Product Manager
- Share user feedback, data, and constraints early; they use this to prioritize.
- Raise trade-off questions with concrete options and impact analysis.
- Align on acceptance criteria before development begins to avoid rework.

### Project Manager
- Keep them informed of blockers, risks, and dependencies as soon as they emerge.
- Use shared project boards and status channels rather than ad-hoc messages.
- Flag timeline risks early so they can replan before commitments are missed.

### Developer
- Provide clear acceptance criteria and design specs before sprint starts.
- Give timely, actionable feedback during code and design reviews.
- Include developers in estimation and design discussions—their input improves quality.

### UX Designer
- Share user research, analytics, and feedback so they can ground designs in data.
- Involve them before implementation starts, not after, to reduce costly rework.
- Use annotated prototypes as the source of truth for interaction behavior.

### QA Lead
- Include testability requirements in acceptance criteria from the start.
- Give QA adequate time in the sprint; don't compress testing to meet arbitrary dates.
- Treat defect reports as quality information, not blame—collaborate on root cause.

### Scrum Master
- Surface blockers in standups rather than holding them until they escalate.
- Be open to process feedback—their coaching goal is the team's long-term health.
- Prepare brief context for retrospective items so discussions stay focused.

### Technical Writer
- Include documentation in the definition of done so it's never an afterthought.
- Review drafts promptly and provide technical corrections directly in the document.
- Share links to relevant source material (PRDs, design specs, API contracts) early.

## Key Questions to Ask Each Role by Project Phase

### Initiation
- **Sponsor**: What are the top two business outcomes this project must deliver?
- **Product Manager**: What does success look like in 6 months, and how will we measure it?
- **Project Manager**: What are the biggest scheduling or dependency risks we should plan for now?

### Planning
- **Developer**: What are the highest-risk technical unknowns, and how can we de-risk them early?
- **UX Designer**: What user research or usability data should inform our design decisions?
- **QA Lead**: What is our test strategy, and are there gaps in coverage we need to address?
- **Technical Writer**: What documentation is needed for release, and when do you need input from the team?

### Execution
- **Scrum Master**: Are there any impediments blocking the team that haven't been surfaced yet?
- **Developer**: Is the acceptance criteria clear enough to build and test against?
- **QA Lead**: Are there defects or quality signals we should address before continuing?

### Release
- **QA Lead**: Are we ready to certify this release, and are there any open risks?
- **Technical Writer**: Is all user-facing and developer documentation complete and reviewed?
- **Product Manager**: Do the delivered features meet the original success criteria?
- **Sponsor**: Are there stakeholder communications or approvals needed before deployment?

### Retrospective
- **Scrum Master**: What process changes will we commit to for the next sprint or release cycle?
- **Project Manager**: What risks materialized that we didn't anticipate, and how do we improve our risk process?

## Common Misconceptions About Roles

| Misconception | Clarification |
|---|---|
| "The PM decides what gets built." | The **Product Manager** decides *what* and *why*; the **Project Manager** manages *when* and *how* delivery happens. |
| "QA is responsible for quality." | Quality is a shared team responsibility. The **QA Lead** provides strategy and certification, but **Developers** and **PdM** are equally accountable. |
| "The Scrum Master is a project manager." | The **Scrum Master** is a team coach and process facilitator, not an authority over scope or schedule. That accountability rests with the **PM**. |
| "Technical Writers work only at the end." | **Technical Writers** should be involved throughout the project to ensure documentation requirements are captured early. |
| "The Sponsor is involved day-to-day." | The **Sponsor** operates at a strategic level and is typically involved only at milestones or for major decisions and escalations. |
| "UX only does visual design." | **UX Designers** lead user research, information architecture, accessibility validation, and interaction design—not just visual styling. |
